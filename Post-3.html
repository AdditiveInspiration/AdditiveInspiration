Design for 3d Printing

3d Printing and Design May 27, 2017

I thought it would be more interesting, now that the 3d printer is working more reliably to try some custom 3d design. It is pretty easy to browse "thingiverse" style websites and download any number of pre-made 3d models. The results are normally fine and you could go your entire life without ever designing anything new or original. I have been experimenting with 3d design software available on the market, and I thought I would detail some of the basics to help other beginners get a little bit more out of their printers!

I have been experimenting with Fusion 360, it is free for students and hobbyists, and it is relatively easy to setup! The link for fusion 360 can be found here

Download Fusion 360 from the link above and we can get started designing some basic shapes!! As you can see from the image above I have been able to model basic text in fusion 360! Its a very small achievement but it feels great to have created something that we could send to 3d printing software for printing.

Open up fusion 360 and get aquainted with the basic interface. Its much easier to create a 3d model when you have a plan and an idea in your head. It can be beneficial to sketch out your design, splitting it into the different shapes that you can then sketch in fusion 360. This first project will be simple and easy for anyone to do. The focus will be on creating a functional model, nothing too artsy! We are going to try and design an led lamp.

Rememeber to check in the settings on the generator website to ensure that negative is not set...otherwise the dark areas will be thinnest and light areas thickest producing a scary gost like effect!

I have found an online lithopane generator - its a simple piece of code which converts digital images into 3d models. Darker areas will be thicker, Lighter areas thinner - when a light is placed near the printed model the image can be seen clearly in grey scale. It is an interesting concept and definitely a great way to showcase a 3d printer! I have 4 of these printed and ready to go - I won't detail how they were generated - it is a simple easy to follow web, found at the link Here

In the settings panel ensure that the lithopane generated is at least 100 mm wide...It can really be any size as long as you remeber the dimensions for later and produce each of the four lithopanes to fit the same dimensions. With the lithopanes generated in .stl we are going to creat a simple holder that the lithopanes can slot into and an lightsource can fit into.

Click on the sketch tab and lets get started. Select center rectangle and click to draw the shape. You can type the dimensions for each side and tab to get to the next dimension. The width of this square should equal the width of the lithopanes you generated, remember to add on a milimetre to account for variations in the printer. If you know your printer esteps (the amount the motor moves with each step) are under or over then account for this in the design. Click sketch again and create a square inside the square you just generated, subtract 10 mm from the lithopane dimension.
Click the blue block icon in the create tab. Select Extrude option...alternatively you can click the ebutton while selecting the face of the sketch. Select the outer square, It should highlight the outer square minus the inner square. Click the e button and select to extrude the box by 10 mm. This will create a solid square frame which will form the base of our lamp! This time we want to sketch on the solid surface we have just created.
Sketch four new rectangles, this time selecting two point rectangles and shift click each one to extrude. The value you extrude by should be the same as the height of the lithopanes you created.
The main structure is now in place, it is simple but it really shows how easy it is to use the basic shapes provided by fusion 360 to create digital, functional models! The next step is slightly more complicated..it will help us use a very useful feature of fusion 360. Click l and draw a line mid way through the model. Zoom into one of the posts and draw another rectangle which cuts into each post as shown below.
Click on the sketch face. Click the sketch menu again and select mirror. Select each line of the rectangle and then in the contect menu select one of the lines you drew. This will project the pattern to the other side of the model. Repeat using the second mirror line until the same sketch is projected to each of the four posts. When sketches become intricate and detailed this feature becomes very useful. Shift select each four rectangles and click e to extrude. This time input a minus number and select cut in the context/extrude menu.
The structure is made so that the lithopanes can slide and clamp in between the brackets. Its not pretty but it is a good way to practice some beginner modelling. To make it a little bit more easy on the eyes select the modify tab and select the fillet option. Select the outer edges of the rectangle and click ok, the edges of teh rectangular frame will be a little bit less angular and a bit more pleasing on the eye!
To print the part on your printer, simply find the object in the browser (to the left by default in fusion 360), right click on the body and select save as stl. This file can be imported into Cura and printed on your 3d printer!!
